Chapter 1 — The Warming

By mid-century, entire continents warm by 3–4°C

The Arctic warms 2–4× faster than the global average — a phenomenon called polar amplification. Inland regions and the tropics face persistent heat that ecosystems and infrastructure weren't designed for.

Chapter 1 — The Warming

By 2100, the Arctic is unrecognizable

Under SSP5-8.5, CanESM5 projects 7°C+ global warming by 2100 — one of the highest but physically plausible estimates among CMIP6 models. The Arctic exceeds 12°C above pre-industrial baseline.

This isn't a prediction. It's what's at stake if we do nothing.

Chapter 2 — The Feedback Loops

Sea ice is disappearing — and that makes everything worse

Arctic sea ice reflects 80% of sunlight back to space. As it melts, dark ocean absorbs that heat instead. This is the albedo feedback loop — warming causes ice loss which causes more warming.

Chapter 2 — The Feedback Loops

By 2060, summer sea ice has nearly vanished under high emissions

What took 10,000 years to freeze is melting in decades. The cascade doesn't stop at the poles — disrupted Arctic circulation destabilizes weather patterns across North America, Europe, and Asia.

~0% summer ice Arctic September sea-ice extent by 2060s under SSP5-8.5
Chapter 2 — The Feedback Loops

A self-reinforcing cycle with no natural brake

Ice loss → more absorbed heat → more warming → more ice loss. Each feedback amplifies the next. This is why the climate system responds more than linearly to emissions — and why high-sensitivity models like CanESM5 matter for understanding risk.

Chapter 3 — The Rainfall Collapse

Warming rewrites where rain falls

A warmer atmosphere holds more water vapor — but it doesn't distribute it evenly. As global circulation patterns shift, rainfall concentrates in some regions and abandons others entirely.

Chapter 3 — The Rainfall Collapse

"Wet gets wetter, dry gets drier" — but unequally

Blue regions receive more rain than their historical average. Brown regions lose it. The Amazon dries. The Sahel dries. Mediterranean Europe dries. These are agricultural and water-supply crises in the making.

−30% rainfall Projected loss in parts of Sub-Saharan Africa and the Amazon by 2090s
Chapter 3 — The Rainfall Collapse

The regions losing rain are already food-insecure

Drought doesn't just reduce harvests — it depletes groundwater, forces livestock losses, and triggers migration. By end of century under high emissions, these pressures become structural, not seasonal.

Chapter 4 — The Crush

Some regions face all three crises at once

Extreme heat, collapsing rainfall, and rising seas don't arrive separately. Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and Southeast Asia face all three simultaneously — a compounding crisis that each individual variable understates.

Chapter 4 — The Crush

Wet-bulb temperature: the silent killer

When heat and humidity combine, the body can no longer cool itself by sweating. Above 31°C wet-bulb, outdoor labor becomes unsafe. Above 35°C, even shade and water can't prevent death.

Under high emissions, large parts of South Asia, the Persian Gulf, and West Africa cross these thresholds for weeks each year by 2070.

35°C wet-bulb The physiological survival limit — regardless of shade or hydration
Chapter 4 — The Crush

The regions hit hardest contributed the least

Sub-Saharan Africa accounts for less than 4% of historical cumulative CO₂ emissions. South Asia, roughly 7%. Yet both face the most compounded climate impacts. This is the central injustice of climate change — the burden is inverted from the cause.

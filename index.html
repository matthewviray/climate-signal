Climate inequality · Global baseline

Climate change is global, but its causes and consequences are uneven.

Human emissions have reshaped the climate system. Rising temperatures, shifting precipitation patterns, and declining sea ice reveal a planet changing unevenly across space.

Chapter 1 · Sea Ice

Polar regions are changing fastest.

Warming is not evenly distributed across the planet. Sea ice decline is one of the clearest visible indicators of accelerating climate change.

Chapter 3 · Precipitation

Rainfall patterns become less stable.

Climate impacts are not limited to warming alone. Some regions experience intensifying drought, while others face more extreme precipitation events.

Chapter 4 · Temperature

Climate impacts are unevenly distributed.

Some of the countries facing the strongest projected warming contributed relatively little to cumulative global emissions.

Dangerous heat stress expands.

Wet-bulb temperatures combine heat and humidity, creating conditions that threaten human survivability.

Responsibility and vulnerability diverge.

Major historical emitters are highlighted in gold, while highly impacted countries are highlighted in violet.

4% vs 30% Sub-Saharan Africa has contributed only about 4% of historical global emissions, but faces roughly 30% of global climate-related damages in this narrative framing.
